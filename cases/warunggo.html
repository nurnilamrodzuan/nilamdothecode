01 Overview

What WarungGo is, in one breath

WarungGo is a two-part product: a customer mobile app for browsing a menu and placing orders, and a real-time web dashboard the kitchen uses to receive, track, and complete those orders without ever picking up the phone.

It was built for a single family-run restaurant first, then shaped into something three more outlets now use daily.

Cover image / hero mockup
02 The challenge

Orders were getting lost between the phone and the kitchen

During peak hours, staff juggled WhatsApp messages, phone calls, and walk-ins on scraps of paper. Orders were missed, repeated, or rung up wrong — and there was no record of anything afterwards.

Before

The pain points

  • Orders taken over 3 different channels, none of them logged
  • Kitchen overwhelmed and frequently lost track during rush hour
  • No daily sales record, so stock planning was guesswork
  • Customers calling repeatedly to check order status
After

What I built to fix it

  • One mobile app where customers order and pay in two taps
  • A live kitchen dashboard that updates the second an order lands
  • Every order stored, with an auto-generated daily sales summary
  • Push notifications keep customers updated — no more calls
03 Process

How it came together

1

Sat in the restaurant for two days

Watched a real lunch rush to map exactly where orders broke down before writing a single line of code.

2

Wireframed the two flows separately

The customer's job (order fast) and the kitchen's job (never miss one) are different, so I designed them as two distinct experiences sharing one backend.

3

Built the real-time core first

WebSockets push new orders to the kitchen instantly. I proved this worked end-to-end before building anything pretty around it.

4

Tested live with one outlet

Ran it through real service for two weeks, fixed what broke, and only then rolled it out to other restaurants.

05 What I learned

The hardest part wasn't the code

Watching the kitchen during a real rush taught me more than any spec ever could. The first design I sketched looked clean but would have slowed the staff down — the dashboard needed fewer taps and bigger touch targets than I assumed. If I rebuilt it today, I'd start by observing the people who use it, every single time.
